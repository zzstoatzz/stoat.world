"The Talisman" by Stephen King and Peter Straub

The Talisman was the first King-involved book I've read in a while (really, the first full novel I've read recently). It's riveting, althoughly slightly of the same kin as The Series of Unfortunate Event books -- where gaudy misfortune presents itself reliably to the main characters at almost every flip of scenery. Despite this minorly exhausting aspect, which is my only true complaint, I did greatly enjoy the characters. Jack, the extraordinarily witty and mature for his age, and Speedy, old and wizened -- you can literally hear when he says "Yeah-bob". I really got immersed in the last couple days of reading because the climax spanned about 60-80 pages. While predictable, it was an extremely interesting concept with which King and Straub took no shortcut to explain, though for the better of the story (imo). As always with King, I'm astounded at his ability to make even the most stauch realist fear something at night. Barren and grimy Oatley, the human flytrap, will make me think twice before I stop along the freeway to fill up on gas; Sunlight Gardener and his "home" for the boys who stray from righteousness stokes my preconcieved unease with cult-like fanantics, Sloat's cold-hearted ambition and its resulting terrors incite worries of crazy-ass people in industries of my future employment, and the chicken farmer who gave Jack a lift just weirds me out (also reminds me that social interaction is pretty nice sometimes). King makes you doubt your ability to see evil in everyday life with how he constructs such layered and malevolent characters in such a relatable context. A great read.
