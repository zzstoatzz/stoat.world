"The Talisman" by Stephen King and Peter Straub

The Talisman was the first King-involved book I've read in a while (really, the first full novel I've read recently). It's riveting, althoughly slightly of the same kin as The Series of Unfortunate Event books -- where gaudy misfortune presents itself reliably to the main characters at almost every flip of scenery. Despite this minorly exhausting aspect, which is my only true complaint, I did greatly enjoy the characters. Jack, the extraordinarily witty and mature for his age, and Speedy, old and wizened -- you can literally hear when he says "Yeah-bob". I really got immersed in the last couple days of reading because the climax spanned about 60-80 pages. While predictable, it was an extremely interesting concept with which King and Straub took no shortcut to explain, though for the better of the story (imo). As always with King, I'm astounded at his ability to make even the most stauch realist fear something at night. Barren and grimy Oatley, the human flytrap, will make me think twice before I stop along the freeway to fill up on gas; Sunlight Gardener and his "home" for the boys who stray from righteousness stokes my preconcieved unease with cult-like fanantics, Sloat's cold-hearted ambition and its resulting terrors incite worries of crazy-ass people in industries of my future employment, and the chicken farmer who gave Jack a lift just weirds me out (also reminds me that social interaction is pretty nice sometimes). King makes you doubt your ability to see evil in everyday life with how he constructs such layered and malevolent characters in such a relatable context. A great read.

"1984" by George Orwell

1984 was a book that I started to read a few years ago, back when the main attraction of reading a book was to glean its exciting features. I didn't have any sort of political philosophy when I was 13, nor do I really have one at this time, but I now have the capacity to at least appreciate someone's political motives and how they manifest themselves in literature. The reason I threw the word politics out there right away is obvious if you've ever read the book. Orwell's piece de resistance is a critique of English socialism and Russian communism. At 19 years of age and nearly 80 years removed from the climate in which Orwell wrote 1984, this basic level is the extent of my true understanding of its allusion. However, there are many statements made by Orwell which require little historical background of 20th century political ideologies. For example, the most striking parts of the story for me occur when the eventual antagonist pulls the classic "I'm the villian and you're fucked so I'm going to tell you my evil plan" on the protagonist. This is where many of Orwells fierce resentments for the current state of politics rear their collective head as what appears to be a general misanthropy. To sort of switch gears though and deconstruct the tangibles of the book rather than its sociopolitical significance, I sometimes felt confused by the actions of Winston. Sometimes statements were made that suggested that he was eternally conditioned by the party to act in a certain way, but then you would find him defying these conditionings as the "stain" in the Party, but it never really seemed to be explained why he was a special case. I'm not sure, those are my thoughts. It's extremely thought provoking, I RECOMMEND!
